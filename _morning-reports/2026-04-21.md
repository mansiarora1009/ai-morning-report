## Top 3 Things Worth Paying Attention To

### 1. Kimi K2.6 — the best free model for agentic coding just got a major upgrade
**What it is:** Moonshot released Kimi K2.6, an open-weight 1T-parameter MoE (32B active, 384 experts, 256K context, native multimodality, INT4 quant) with day-0 support in vLLM, OpenRouter, Cloudflare Workers AI, MLX, Hermes Agent, and OpenCode. Claims open-source SOTA on SWE-Bench Pro (58.6), HLE w/ tools (54.0), and BrowseComp (83.2). The more interesting headline: 4,000+ tool calls in a single run, 12+ hour continuous operation, 300 parallel sub-agents, and "Claw Groups" for multi-agent coordination. Community is already reporting successful 5-day autonomous infra agent runs and kernel rewrites using it as a Claude/GPT backend drop-in.

**Why it matters:** The question is no longer "can open models match closed models on benchmarks." Kimi K2.6 is demonstrably capable enough to run real agentic coding workloads — for free, open-weight, deployable locally. The story for Mansi's audience: frontier-level coding agents just became accessible to anyone who can spin up vLLM. If you've been relying on Claude or GPT as the only capable backbone for agents, there's now a serious alternative with no API costs.

### 2. OpenAI Codex Chronicle — your AI coding tool is starting to watch your screen
**What it is:** OpenAI shipped Codex Chronicle (research preview for Pro on macOS, excluding EU/UK/Switzerland): background agents capture screenshots of your work context, build structured memories from them, store everything on-device, and let you inspect/edit those memories. Codex then draws on this ambient history when helping you. Rolling out gradually. @hwchase17 (LangChain's founder) noted immediately: "memory will be the great lock-in."

**Why it matters:** This is a meaningful shift from "AI that knows your current chat" to "AI that knows your working style, project context, and history." The practical upshot is that the AI tool you keep using longest will become the most useful — not because it's the smartest model, but because it has the richest memory of your work. For people who use multiple AI coding tools, this is the moment to think clearly about where you're building your context.

### 3. Anthropic used Claude to automate AI safety research — the AI beat the humans
**What it is:** Anthropic published results from their Automated Alignment Researcher (AAR) project: teams of Claude Opus 4.6 agents working in parallel sandboxes, sharing findings on a forum, proposing hypotheses, running experiments, and iterating autonomously on weak-to-strong supervision research. Two human researchers spent 7 days and achieved a Performance Gap Recovered (PGR) score of 0.23. The AARs ran for 5 more days (800 cumulative agent-hours, $18K in compute) and hit PGR 0.97 — nearly closing the entire gap, nearly 4x the human baseline. The method also generalized to new math datasets (PGR 0.94).

**Why it matters:** This is the "AI doing AI research" story going from theoretical to demonstrated. The bottleneck they identified: the hard part isn't running experiments — it's designing the evals. A human still had to assign initial research directions; without that, the agents converged on too few ideas. But within a scoped problem with clear metrics, Claude Opus 4.6 agents outperformed human researchers on a real alignment task. The implication for Mansi's audience: the AI tools you're building with are now being improved by other AI agents, not just human engineers.

**Format:** Explainer

**Reel angle:** "Anthropic used Claude to do its own AI safety research — and the AI beat the humans. Here's what that actually means"
